Phase 1: The "Why" & The Commitment Reality Check

Before looking at college rosters, you must evaluate the day-to-day reality of a high school player successfully recruiting for college. Below is an analysis of the weekly schedule and commitment required of top-tier recruits during their sophomore, junior, and senior years.

The Weekly Commitment Analysis

To earn a roster spot at the college level, training doesn't start and end with your club team practices. Successful recruits treat soccer like a craft, dedicating an average of 15 to 22 hours per week to athletic development.

Here is how those hours typically break down each week:

+-----------------------------------------------------------------+
|   Weekly Recruit Time Budget (Total: ~15 to 22 Hours/Week)      |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------+
| [■■■■■■■] Team/Trainer/Skill Work (6-8 hrs)                     |
| [■■■■]    Individual Technical / Wall Work (3-4 hrs)            |
| [■■■]     Strength, Speed & Injury Prevention (2-3 hrs)         |
| [■■■■]    Weekend Games & Active Recovery (3-4 hrs)             |
| [■■■■]    Travel Time to regional events (4-10+ hrs)            |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------+

1. Technical Volume (The "Touches" Metric)

Successful recruits do not rely solely on team practices to develop their skills. They engage in daily self-directed technical work.

  • Daily Target: Minimum of 1,000 to 1,500 purposeful touches per day outside of team training.
  • What this looks like: 20 to 30 minutes of daily wall-ball patterns, individual juggling variations, and quick-footwork drills (cone work).
  • Weekly Total: Approximately 7,000 to 10,000 touches per week.

2. Physical & Athletic Development

College soccer is incredibly physical, fast, and demanding. College coaches recruit athletes first, then soccer players.

  • Strength & Conditioning: 2 to 3 sessions per week (45 to 60 minutes each) focusing on core strength, single-leg stability, explosive power, and ACL injury prevention.
  • Aerobic/Anaerobic Fitness: At least 1 to 2 sessions of speed/endurance work per week, especially during the off-season.

3. Travel & Showcase Logistics

Because you play in a regional league and participate in ODP, travel is a major time commitment.

  • Drive Time: 4 to 10 hours of travel per week for regional away games, state training sessions, and national showcase events.
  • Sacrifice: This often means studying in the car, missing high school social events (dances, football games, hanging out with friends), and eating meals on the go.

Reflection Questions

Question 1: The High School Commitment Check

Prompt: Am I excited by the idea of dedicated daily individual training (1,000+ touches a day) and athletic training, or does this feel like a chore that will burn me out? What am I willing to deprioritize in my life to make room for this commitment?

Question 2: The College Commitment Check

Prompt: Do I love the game of soccer enough to make it the central pillar of my college experience, even if it means missing out on traditional "college social life" experiences?

Question 3: The Role Reality Check

Prompt: Would I be happier sitting on the bench at a famous, high-level Division I school, or playing 90 minutes a game as an impactful leader at a highly competitive Division III or NAIA school?

Question 4: The "Broken Leg" Test

Prompt: If soccer was completely taken away from me, would I still be genuinely happy attending this college for its campus, academics, majors, and student body?

Phase 2: Defining Your School Priorities

If you decided in Phase 1 that you are all-in on college soccer, you now need to figure out what kind of school fits you best. Because you are a high-achieving student taking Arrowhead's most rigorous AP courses, you have immense academic leverage.

Even if you don't know exactly what you want to study yet, you must protect your academic options.

Step A: The Undecided Scholar's Interest Audit & ABS

It is completely normal to be undecided at 15 years old! However, you can likely narrow your general interests down to a few fields.

How we use this: The "Academic Breadth Score" (ABS)

Because you are a high-achieving student who is currently undecided, you want to target schools that give you the freedom to choose your major later. Once you have selected up to 5 interest areas below, we will use them to grade any prospective college on a scale of 0 to 5.

  • Score 5/5: The school has strong, dedicated majors in all 5 of your interest areas.
  • Score 3/5 or 4/5: The school has most of your interests, representing a solid academic fit.
  • Score 0/5 to 2/5: Red Flag. If you choose this school and your current top interest changes, you may be stuck without a major you like.
